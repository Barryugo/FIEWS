Food Inflation Early Warning System

Which variables lead Nigeria's food inflation — and by how many months?
Key Finding

Nigeria's food inflation has a detectable 3–6 month early warning window. Exchange rate shocks, fuel price surges, and global food price movements consistently precede domestic inflation spikes. A monitoring framework tracking these indicators can provide policymakers with actionable lead time.

Executive Summary
  • Nigeria's food inflation is highly persistent: once an episode begins, it typically takes 12–18 months to unwind, making early detection more valuable than crisis response.
  • There is a detectable 3–6 month early warning window driven by exchange rate movements, fuel price shocks, and global food price changes — all of which precede domestic inflation spikes in the historical record.
  • Current food CPI of 16.1% (April 2026) sits in the elevated risk zone. The 6-month model projects a rise to 20.8%, warranting active monitoring even as headline inflation continues to decline from its 2024 peak.
  • The ARIMA model achieves a 3-month MAE of 1.62 percentage points. The Gradient Boosting model provides the most policy-useful output: probability scores, driver attribution, and scenario analysis.
Section 01
01

Background and Motivation

Food inflation in Nigeria is not a routine macroeconomic variable. It directly determines household welfare for the majority of Nigerians who spend 50–60% of income on food, it drives political instability, and it imposes compounding costs on fiscal and monetary policy when it becomes entrenched. The 2023–2024 episode — in which food inflation reached 40.9% — was the most severe in the country's recent history, erasing real income gains across multiple income deciles simultaneously.

Despite this, Nigeria lacks a systematic early warning framework. The National Bureau of Statistics publishes monthly CPI data with a 2–3 week lag; the Central Bank monitors food prices indirectly through headline inflation; and policy responses to food price surges have historically been reactive rather than anticipatory. This project addresses that gap.

Which economic variables consistently precede food inflation in Nigeria — and by how many months? The answer determines when to intervene, through what channels, and at what cost.

Data and Variables

2.1 — Target Variable

The target is Nigeria's food CPI year-on-year percentage change, sourced from the National Bureau of Statistics. The series covers May 2012 to April 2026 (168 monthly observations). Two forecast targets are constructed: food_cpi shifted back 3 months (target_3m) and shifted back 6 months (target_6m), so that models trained at time t predict the inflation rate at t+3 and t+6 respectively.

Methodology

3.1 — Exploratory Analysis

Cross-correlation analysis was performed across all predictors at lags of −6 to +12 months. The autocorrelation function (ACF) of food CPI yields a half-life of approximately 14 months — autocorrelation remains above 0.5 for over a year. This persistence is the dominant feature of Nigeria's food inflation and is the primary reason the ARIMA model performs so well on short-horizon forecasting.

3.2 — Model Architecture

Four models were trained for each forecast horizon using time-series cross-validation (5 folds, 12-month test windows):

Key Findings

4.1 — Leading Indicators

The cross-correlation analysis reveals a consistent hierarchy of predictors. Among external indicators, the clearest leading signals are fuel prices (3–5 month lead, r = +0.55) and exchange rate movements (3–4 months, r = +0.46).

Two Notable Findings

Fuel prices lead food inflation by 3–5 months (r = +0.55). This reflects Nigeria's transport-intensive food supply chain — commodity movements from northern agricultural states to southern consuming states depend heavily on road freight, which is diesel-dependent.

Exchange rate shocks lead food inflation by 3–4 months (r = +0.46) through the import cost channel: fertilisers, agrochemicals, machinery, and some food products are dollar-priced, and depreciation raises input costs with a lag.

The 2023–2024 Episode

The most instructive case. Fuel subsidy removal (June 2023) triggered a 129% PMS price increase within 3 months. Simultaneous naira floatation caused a 40% official rate depreciation. Diesel prices had already risen 300% over the preceding 12 months. The fuel shock signal was visible 3 months before the CPI spike. A functioning FIEWS would have flagged High Risk by March 2023 — three months before the acute phase.

Why ARIMA Wins — and Why That's Expected

The ARIMA model dominates on MAE (1.62pp for both horizons) because Nigeria's food inflation has a lag-1 autocorrelation of 0.98. Knowing last month's reading is extremely informative for next month's. The ML models contribute value in two areas ARIMA cannot reach: (1) detecting regime changes before they appear in the CPI series itself, by monitoring external shocks; (2) generating probability scores and scenario outputs more useful to policymakers than point forecasts.

Structural Insight from Scenario Analysis

The model is relatively insensitive to individual shocks in the near term because inflation momentum features dominate the 3-month signal. This is economically correct — it reflects the high persistence of Nigeria's inflation. The policy implication is that the most important intervention window is during the lag period itself: acting when the shock occurs, not when it appears in the CPI data.

Section 07
07

Policy Recommendations

7.1 — Immediate Monitoring Priorities

Based on the lead-lag findings, the following four variables should form the core of any early warning dashboard, with explicit trigger thresholds:

Monitor 01

FX Rate — Monthly

A 3-month depreciation exceeding 10% should trigger Elevated alert status. This indicator has the most reliable 3–4 month lead time in all four historical episodes.

Trigger: 3m depreciation >10%
Monitor 02

Diesel Price — Monthly

A 3-month increase exceeding 15% warrants immediate review. The 2022 diesel surge was the single clearest precursor to the 2023–2024 food inflation crisis.

Trigger: 3m increase >15%
Monitor 03

FAO Cereals Index — Monthly

Available the first Friday of each month. A 3-month increase exceeding 10% warrants Elevated status, particularly if coinciding with FX pressure.

Trigger: 3m increase >10%
Monitor 04

Rainfall Anomaly — Monthly

Deficits exceeding 20% below seasonal average during April–September planting and growing seasons should trigger supply-side alerts.

Trigger: anomaly <−20% (Apr–Sep)

7.2 — Institutional Recommendations

  • 01Establish a Food Inflation Monitoring Unit within the NBS or CBN that produces monthly FIEWS risk scores and publishes them alongside the CPI release.
  • 02Pre-position buffer stock triggers: strategic grain reserve releases should be authorised to begin when the 3-month risk score exceeds 40% — not after CPI confirms the spike.
  • 03Link fertiliser subsidy release schedules to the rainfall anomaly indicator. Poor planting season signals (anomaly <−20%) should trigger accelerated distribution.
  • 04Coordinate FX intervention with food inflation signals: CBN stabilisation operations should be explicitly linked to the FIEWS depreciation flag to interrupt the import cost transmission channel.

7.3 — Model Development Priorities

  • 01Integrate parallel market exchange rate data — the official rate understated actual FX pressure throughout 2022–2023 and delayed signal detection.
  • 02Extend rainfall data post-2020 using satellite sources (CHIRPS or PERSIANN) to replace the seasonal mean extrapolation used in this analysis.
  • 03Incorporate food import volume data (NCS trade data) as an additional leading indicator. Import compression during FX crises is a mechanism not yet captured by the current feature set.
  • 04Retrain models quarterly as new CPI data arrives. The 2023–2024 regime was structurally different from prior episodes and the training window should reflect new structural parameters.
Section 08
08

Limitations

  • L1Panel size: 168 observations (14 years) is sufficient for monthly time-series analysis but limits the number of complete inflation cycles available for training. The model has observed four major episodes; unusual combinations of drivers outside historical experience may not be captured.
  • L2Missing fertiliser data: World Bank Pink Sheet commodity prices were available only at annual and quarterly frequency and could not be incorporated. Urea and DAP fertiliser price signals — potentially important leading indicators for agricultural input costs — are absent from the current feature set.
  • L3Extrapolated rainfall: CHIRPS coverage ends December 2020. Rainfall data for 2021–2026 is extrapolated from seasonal means, reducing the rainfall variable's predictive value for the most recent period.
  • L4ARIMA approximation: The ARIMA benchmark is an AR(6) model via Ridge regression, used because the statsmodels library was unavailable in this environment. A full ARIMA(p,d,q) with automatic order selection would be the appropriate benchmark in a production setting.
09 — Conclusion

The Early Warning Window Is Open

Nigeria's food inflation is predictable — not in the sense of point-forecast precision, but in the sense that the economic preconditions for inflation crises are consistently observable 3–6 months before they appear in the CPI data. Exchange rate depreciation, fuel price surges, and global food price shocks are not merely correlated with food inflation: they precede it, they cause it, and they do so through identifiable transmission channels.

The Food Inflation Early Warning System provides a replicable, data-driven framework for monitoring those channels. With monthly updates to six data series and a 30-minute model rerun, it can produce a risk score, a probability distribution, and a scenario analysis that give policymakers the lead time they need to intervene before crises become entrenched.

The April 2026 reading — 16.1%, Elevated risk, 6-month forecast of 20.8% — is not alarming in isolation. But it is a reminder that Nigeria remains structurally exposed to food inflation shocks, and that the current disinflation should not be mistaken for structural stability.

The early warning window is open. Whether it is used is a question of institutional will, not analytical capacity.
